Private Pilot Licence • Singapore Youth Flying Club

1.5 years of flight training that changed the way I think under pressure — not just in the cockpit, but in everything I do after.

Aviation
  • Logged nearly 80 flight hours through dual sorties, solo flights, emergency scenarios, and a final test I cleared one week before my JC2 prelims.
  • Passed 7 CAAS aviation theory exams alongside 5 A-Level subjects, cricket, and leadership duties — there was no slack in the schedule.
  • The hardest lesson flying taught me was not procedural. It was that calm is something you build deliberately, one difficult situation at a time.

Engineers Innovation Challenge 2023 • Top 10 Nationwide

We built an AI device for elderly people living alone — not because it was assigned, but because the problem was real and we felt responsible for trying.

Engineering
  • Designed a table-mounted AI assistant that detected emotional cues through voice, aimed at reducing isolation for elderly people living alone.
  • Worked through months of coding, hardware integration, and design constraints as a team — the prototype had to work, not just look like it worked.
  • Being selected for the World Engineers Summit made me realise that even as a student, you can work on problems that genuinely matter.

Raffles Entrepreneurs Network • Member of the Year

EntreWeek taught me something I did not expect — that telling people what to do is easy, but making them care is the actual work of leadership.

Leadership
  • Spearheaded EntreWeek, a school-wide week-long event on sustainable entrepreneurship — liaising with speakers, vendors, and a team with competing ideas under a tight deadline.
  • When my team pushed me to make all decisions myself to speed things up, I pushed back — and split the team into groups that owned their parts of the event.
  • We engaged 85% of the student population that week. More importantly, every person on the team felt it was theirs.

WebForce • Android App for Magicians

It started with a friend who needed a tool that did not exist. It became a product used by performers in 10 countries who trust it on stage.

Product Building
  • Identified a real gap — iOS had tools for phone-based magic performance, Android had nothing reliable. Built the first version to solve one person's problem.
  • Iterated through live performer feedback until the app was trustworthy enough for a stage where failure in front of an audience is not an option.
  • 60+ installs across 10 countries, SGD 1,700 in revenue, now scaling through a UK distributor. The numbers are not the point — the trust is.

Teaching, Mentoring, and Giving Back

A student I had never met commented on my YouTube video saying my explanation finally made circles click for them. That comment stayed with me longer than most awards have.

Service
  • Started a YouTube channel teaching PSLE Maths and Science after realising that good tuition was inaccessible to many students who needed it most.
  • Mentored Primary 6 students at my own alma mater and through community centres — re-explaining the same concept in seven different ways until one of them finally lands.
  • Teaching changed the way I think about leadership. Patience, not authority, is what earns you the right to guide someone.

ExpertConnect Asia Internship

I was also doing SYFC flight training at the time. My supervisor wrote in my reference letter that despite that, I was never once late or disengaged. That is probably the detail I am most proud of.

Industry Exposure
  • Led development of a landing site for BizConnect, conducted market research using TAM-SAM-SOM frameworks, and overhauled the company website — all within two weeks.
  • Navigated shifting requirements, ambiguous briefs, and fast-changing priorities without waiting to be told what to do next.
  • Left with confirmation that I thrive in environments where initiative is expected and the problems are genuinely unsolved.

What I Actually Think

The ideas that connect everything above.

What flying really taught me

My instructor once cut the throttle mid-flight and said "engine failure." Everything went quiet — the engine, the cockpit, my thoughts. Then training took over. What stayed with me was not the checklist. It was the realisation that in genuinely high-stakes situations, fear is not useful. Only thinking is.

Why engineering, specifically

Engineering is what turns an idea into something a person can actually depend on. The aircraft that kept me safe at 3,000 feet was not a concept — it was precision, tested and retested. I want to build things with that same standard of reliability.

Why I build things rather than just study them

WebForce taught me the difference between something that works in theory and something a performer trusts on stage in front of 200 people. That gap — between functional and reliable — is where the real engineering happens. I want to live in that gap.

What I am looking for in a programme

I do not want to learn engineering in isolation from the world it has to operate in. The problems I care about — safer systems, better human-machine interaction, technologies that reach people who need them — do not sit neatly inside a single discipline. REP does not ask me to pretend they do.

Why NTU REP

Not because it is prestigious. Because it is the right fit.

Every experience I have described above sits at the intersection of more than one domain. Flying is engineering and decision-making and human psychology. WebForce is software and business and user trust. EntreWeek is leadership and communication and logistics. REP is the first programme I have encountered that is built around that exact intersection — and that is why I am here.

Why REP fits the way I already think

  • The engineering depth matches the standards I have already held myself to — CAAS exams, national competitions, live product builds.
  • The business and entrepreneurship dimension is not new to me — it is the lens I have used to turn problems into products since JC.
  • The global immersion component matters because the problems I want to work on are not Singaporean problems — they are human ones.
  • REP's interdisciplinary structure mirrors something I have learnt the hard way: real problems do not respect the boundaries between subjects.

What I will bring to REP

  • A genuine comfort with high-stakes pressure — not claimed, but built over 76 hours in the air and a family crisis survived quietly during exam season.
  • A habit of building things that have to work in the real world, not just pass a test.
  • A leadership style that creates ownership in the people around me, not just direction.
  • A belief that engineering earns its meaning when it serves people who actually need it.
